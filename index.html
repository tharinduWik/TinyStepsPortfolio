About TinySteps

Infant monitoring is fragmented by design.

The first two years of life are the most sensitive window for physical and neurodevelopmental growth — yet routine monitoring is limited to infrequent clinical visits and single-domain apps that address one health dimension at a time.

The Problem

Simple charts, single-domain screens, unvalidated advice.

Existing mobile health applications for infant care fall into three categories: simple growth chart visualisation tools, single-domain screening instruments, and unvalidated caregiver advice platforms. None of these integrates predictive analytics, behavioural assessment and maternal health monitoring into a unified, AI-driven system.

Caregivers and clinicians alike are left stitching signals together by hand — missed anomalies, late interventions, inconsistent follow-up.

Our Solution

Four clinically-informed modules, one mobile platform.

TinySteps is a four-module AI infant monitoring system: LSTM-based growth forecasting with XGBoost risk prediction, a two-stage Q-CHAT ASD screening pipeline, a multi-modal audio-visual cry analyser, and an ensemble postpartum maternal health assessor.

Every model is trained on a purpose-specific dataset, deployed through a FastAPI backend with a React Native mobile frontend, and lightweight enough for consumer mobile devices.

Literature Review

The foundation of our research.

A survey of pediatric AI, developmental screening, cry analysis and postpartum monitoring — and the gaps each domain leaves unanswered.

01

Growth monitoring & predictive analytics

Recent machine learning approaches apply Random Forests and gradient boosting to malnutrition classification (Pérez-Rosales et al., 2021) and childhood stunting prediction (Hasan et al., 2020). However, most focus on cross-sectional snapshots rather than longitudinal trajectory modelling. LSTM networks have demonstrated value in time-series pediatric health monitoring (Che et al., 2018) but prior deployments use large architectures unsuitable for mobile applications.

References
  1. Pérez-Rosales, A. K. et al. (2021). Machine learning for malnutrition classification in children under five. PLoS ONE.
  2. Hasan, T. et al. (2020). Prediction of childhood stunting using machine learning. BMC Public Health.
  3. Che, H. et al. (2018). Recurrent neural networks for multivariate time series with missing values. Scientific Reports.
02

ASD screening instruments & ML layers

ASD screening tools have been extensively studied in clinical research using the M-CHAT (Robins et al., 2001) and Q-CHAT (Allison et al., 2008) instruments. Mobile adaptations exist but rarely incorporate machine learning classification layers for borderline cases. Prior work (Thabtah et al., 2019) has shown that combining behavioural feature vectors with gradient boosting classifiers can improve screening sensitivity in community settings.

References
  1. Robins, D. S. et al. (2001). Screening for autism in toddlers. Dev. Med. Child Neurol.
  2. Allison, T. et al. (2008). The Q-CHAT (Quantitative Checklist for Autism in Toddlers). JADD.
  3. Thabtah, F. et al. (2019). A machine learning autism classification based on logistic regression. Health Inf. Sci. Syst.
03

Multi-modal infant cry analysis

Infant cry analysis has been approached through audio classification using CNNs on mel spectrograms (Chang et al., 2019) and SVMs on MFCC features (Lavner et al., 2018). Multi-modal systems combining audio and facial expression cues have shown improved accuracy (Zhang et al., 2022) but have not previously been integrated into a unified infant monitoring application.

References
  1. Chang, C. Y. et al. (2019). Infant cry classification using deep learning. ICASSP.
  2. Lavner, S. et al. (2018). Infant cry analysis and detection. Comput. Biol. Med.
  3. Zhang, Y. et al. (2022). Multi-modal infant cry recognition combining audio and facial features. IEEE Trans. Affect. Comput.
04

Postpartum maternal recovery

Postpartum health monitoring has received limited attention in AI-assisted mobile health (Declercq et al., 2007), despite the substantial burden of postpartum complications including perineal trauma, C-section recovery, and musculoskeletal pain. Ensemble methods such as Random Forests have been applied to postpartum depression screening (Rai et al., 2021) but not to physical recovery risk stratification across multiple domains simultaneously.

References
  1. Declercq, S. et al. (2007). Listening to mothers: experiences of women after childbirth. J. Midwifery Womens Health.
  2. Rai, M. et al. (2021). Postpartum depression prediction using machine learning. Inform. Med. Unlocked.
Research Gaps

Four gaps, one unified answer.

Longitudinal blind spot

Existing growth apps visualise WHO charts but do not forecast. TinySteps applies a lightweight LSTM (23,298 params) for 7-day weight and height forecasting.

Borderline ASD cases

Single-pass screeners miss nuance. Our two-stage Q-CHAT pipeline escalates borderline (confidence<0.70) cases to an XGBoost second-opinion model.

Audio-only cry engines

Noisy environments break audio-only classifiers. TinySteps fuses MFCC audio with facial pain CNN features through a calibrated fusion model.

Maternal recovery neglect

No mobile AI simultaneously covers back/pelvic pain, perineal trauma and C-section recovery. TinySteps does — with per-domain models.

Our Objectives

Five pillars, one platform.

The TinySteps objectives define the full scope of the project — from predictive growth analytics to behavioural screening and maternal recovery.

01

Lightweight longitudinal forecasting

Deploy a single-layer LSTM (23,298 params, 0.089 MB) to predict next-day weight and height change from a 7-day sliding window of 24 features — mobile-deployable on consumer devices.

02

Early risk detection at low false-alarm cost

An XGBoost classifier with scale_pos_weight=5.85 flags 7-day WAZ crossings of -2.0 with AUC 0.9499 and only 1.63 false alarms per infant — three-fold fewer than the RF baseline.

03

Two-stage developmental screening

Stage-1 logistic regression on 25 Q-CHAT features followed by Stage-2 XGBoost for borderline cases (confidence<0.70), reaching macro AUC 0.912 and at-risk sensitivity 0.88.

04

Multi-modal cry interpretation

Combine MFCC-based audio ensembles with a facial-pain CNN through a calibrated fusion model — 87.4% overall accuracy, versus 79.2% audio-only and 73.6% facial-only.

05

Postpartum three-domain support

Ridge regression for back/pelvic pain (MAE 0.82, R²=0.74), Random Forests for perineal trauma (AUC 0.891) and C-section recovery (AUC 0.876) — each with stratified recommendations.

TinySteps follows a modular client-server architecture. The backend is implemented in Python using FastAPI, with four dedicated routers — growth_router, asd_router, cry_router, postpartum_router — each serving its module's endpoints. All models are loaded at server startup as persistent in-memory objects. MongoDB stores longitudinal infant records, behavioural logs and maternal assessments.

  • 1Discover. Ground research on pediatric workflows, caregiver needs and clinical screening practice; align with WHO Child Growth Standards.
  • 2Curate. Four purpose-specific datasets — synthetic Sri Lankan longitudinal growth (600 infants), Q-CHAT cohort (1,200), multi-modal cry corpus (4,500), postpartum survey (850 mothers).
  • 3Design. Single-layer LSTM for forecasting, XGBoost for risk, logistic+XGBoost two-stage for ASD, audio-visual fusion for cry, Ridge+RF ensembles for postpartum.
  • 4Develop. React Native (Expo) frontend · FastAPI backend · MongoDB persistence · models loaded as in-memory objects at startup.
  • 5Deliver. Sequential pipeline that aggregates risk, anomaly and forecast into a single backend response to minimise round-trips.

Project Roadmap

The journey ahead.

Q1 2026 · Complete

Dataset curation

Synthetic Sri Lankan longitudinal growth (Nethmi + Balita WHO references), Q-CHAT cohort, multi-modal cry corpus and maternal postnatal survey data.

Q2 2026 · Complete

Model training & evaluation

LSTM forecaster, XGBoost risk model, two-stage ASD pipeline, audio-visual cry fusion and postpartum ensembles trained and benchmarked.

Q3 2026 · In progress

Integration & deployment

FastAPI backend with four routers, MongoDB persistence, React Native mobile frontend with five tab screens and module-specific flows.

Q4 2026 · Upcoming

Caregiver pilot

Pilot with caregivers for real-world UX feedback, anomaly-card refinement and calibrated fusion tuning on non-studio cry recordings.

Q1 2027 · Planned

Clinical validation

Prospective validation of growth and postpartum modules on hospital-discharge cohorts. Extension to the full 0–24 month range.

Q2+ 2027 · Vision

Passive & wearable sensing

Integrate passive monitoring via wearable sensors; expand cry model to ambient-noise conditions and add caregiver-worn vitals input.
